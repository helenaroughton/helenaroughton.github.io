Problem Solving

21 September 2025

Blocked on a simple problems

During the FlipFlop problem, which was split into parts, I became stuck for a long time on the first part of the problem. I had already used pseudocode to type/plan out what I needed to do so didn't feel as though redoing this would help. I understood what I needed to do but didn't know how to get there, I spoke aloud, I used google search to try find examples of how to use the statements/arguments/tools I wanted to use, but no matter what I tried I wasn't getting anywhere.

The test errors also weren't really helping me as they kept changing (but not resolving) each time I changed something. I eventually remembered that someone else might have posted in the discord foundations help channel, so I checked this and saw someone had even asked about the same question! Instantly upon checking their question and facilitator corresponding answers, I knew what I was doing wrong. I was trying to incorporate both parts into the first part, and in doing so, completely overcomplicating the answer.

I felt very frustrated before checking discord as I felt as though I was going around in circles. The test was outputting what I needed it to with console.log but the other test errors weren't making much sense in relation to the rest or helping me work out what I was doing wrong. I changed so many different things and never got closer and this never-ending cycle was really challenging to deal with. I was getting super frustrated with myself and the situation. When I finally realised it was because I had overcomplicated it, I was both super relieved, but also disappointed in myself for not realising that sooner or being able to understand that the question was simpler than I thought. I learned that more often than not, the answer is simpler than I think.

Elegantly solved a problem

I quite enjoyed the final problem - deBee. I felt as though the soon as I read the exercise I knew what I needed to do and so wrote this out quickly in pseudocode to get it out of my head before I lost it. I was a bit unsure which method I wanted to use but was more comfortable with one so went with that. While trying to code it I still encountered some problems like not realising that converting all characters to lowercase doesn't action it on the original, which when I found this out, simplified my task. I googled the types of methods trying to find examples of when they were used, and also used an LLM to break down the methods in more detail or to explain what some of the error messages meant.

I felt a mixture of self accomplishment in being able to achieve big parts of the exercise and know what I wanted to do but also confusion when some things didn't work as I expected them to. I learned to not forget to think through the full circle of the exercise. I had forgotten to join the values back into a string from an array and was very confused why it wasn't working but this was the final last step to bring it back full circle.

Reflect on how confident you feel using each of these problem-solving techniques/processes

Pseudocode: If I have an idea of where to start and what methods to use, I like this as a tool to get it out of my head and be able to come back to. But sometimes I didn't even know where to start to even use the pseudocode.

Trying something: This is a go to for me but it was hard or frustrating when you're not even sure if it's close to the right track and whether you're only wrong with syntax or entirely wrong.

Rubber ducky method: I only used this a couple of times and more speaking to myself a couple of the steps rather than the full end to end process so I should try this more going forwards.

Reading error messages: Some of these were easier to read than others and by the end of the exercises, I had started to recognise some of the same types of error messages so got a little better at understanding them. Ultimately, though, I'm not very confident with reading these.

Console.logging: Not confident, every time I try to use console.log it doesn't seem to work. It only worked once for me. I'm not confident when to use, where to enter it, or what to put after it.

Googling: A big go to - especially codefreecamp, W3Schools, MDN, and Stackoverflow! Quite confident with researching terms and methods and trying to find examples of other people doing something similar to me. I'm not so confident reading the answers on forums and understanding what they're saying.

Asking your peers for help: Fine with it! Just haven't had the easiest opportunities to do that while based in UK. The weekly calls have been great for this though.

Asking coaches for help: Similar with above, the timing isn't the best and if it's midday mid problem, it's not always the best solution for me.

Improving your process with reflection: I feel as though I've noticed some improvements in learning through reflection, I think it solidifies a second time a certain technique you've learnt, which becomes easier to draw upon in future. I would like to keep doing the reflections

Reflect on a time you were reluctant to ask for help. Consider what made you reluctant to do so. What might you try differently next time?

I was reluctant to ask for help when I was battling with styling and positions/padding/margins in the second week. I was being stubborn and felt as though struggling with something that early on was embarrassing for me. I so also think the biggest reluctance, which unfortunately is still the case, is the different time zones as help may come 6-12 hours later and by then I'm often so tired it's hard to absorb much more and so it becomes a big barrier. I did, however, ask a question in the next week's homegroup about positioning and it was super helpful! I think for future, I'll ask for help anyway as it will still help no matter when I get it even if I no longer need it in that specific moment.
